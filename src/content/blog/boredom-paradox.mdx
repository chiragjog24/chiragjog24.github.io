Here's an observation I can't shake: the people I know who are most generative — who can spin up ideas, see connections, build things from nothing — are also the ones who struggle most with sustained attention on any single thing.

This isn't about ADHD (though there's overlap). It's about what happens when your brain's idea-generation engine runs faster than any single project can absorb.

## The Overcapacity Hypothesis

Imagine your mind as a factory with production capacity far exceeding demand. The factory doesn't slow down — it can't. So it starts producing for *any* available market. You start a painting, then halfway through you see how it connects to a software architecture pattern, and suddenly you're sketching a database schema on the back of the canvas.

The creative mind doesn't have an off switch. It has a *routing* problem.

## Why This Matters

If you're building a life around creativity — whether that's engineering, art, entrepreneurship, or all three — understanding this pattern changes how you design your days.

The conventional advice is "focus on one thing." But for the overcapacity mind, that's like telling a river to flow through a single pipe. The pressure builds until something breaks.

The alternative: design for **structured variety**. Have three or four active projects at different stages and different domains. When the factory's output exceeds what Project A can absorb, route it to Project B. The key is that the switching is *intentional*, not reactive.

## What I'm Still Working Out

I don't have a clean theory yet — just a pattern I keep noticing. The connection between high creativity and easy boredom feels like it should be obvious, but I haven't found anyone who's formalized it well.

If you've read something that tackles this, I'd love to hear about it.
